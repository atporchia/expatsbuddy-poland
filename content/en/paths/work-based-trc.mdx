If you are a non-EU citizen working in Poland, official sources describe a permit
that combines two things in one decision: the right to stay in Poland and the
right to work for a specific employer. Its formal Polish name is *zezwolenie na
pobyt czasowy i pracę*, and it is commonly called a **single permit** (or
"uniform permit") because it merges the residence part and the work part into
one procedure.

At a high level, two separate questions matter for a foreigner working in
Poland: **is your stay legal** (a residence basis such as a visa or residence
permit) and **is your work legal** (work authorization). The single permit
addresses both at once, which is why it is the path most often mentioned for
employees. Other combinations exist — for example a separate work permit plus
a visa — and the official MOS pages explain when each applies.

The permit is decided by the **voivode** (the head of the voivodeship office)
for the region where you live. Applications are prepared and submitted through
the official **MOS portal**. If the office finds missing formal elements in an
application, it sends a request for filling formal gaps (*wezwanie do
uzupełnienia braków formalnych*) with a deadline set by the office.

Requirements, fees, and document lists change over time and differ between
situations, so treat the official sources below as the reference — especially
the MOS checklists for your specific procedure. If your situation involves a
job change, job loss, or an expiring document, the relevant office or a
qualified professional can confirm what applies to you.

A separate question, distinct from the residence and work permit itself, is
whether your foreign diploma needs to be formally recognized in Poland. Official
sources describe this as depending on the profession, not on nationality:
**regulated professions** (for example doctor, nurse, or architect) generally
require formal recognition, sometimes called diploma nostrification, before
you can practice — this applies to everyone entering that profession, not
only non-EU citizens. For most other, non-regulated jobs, there is no
government recognition step at all, and the employer decides directly whether
to accept your diploma. NAWA's KWALIFIKATOR database can help you check how a
specific foreign diploma is generally regarded.
