Polish citizenship (*obywatelstwo polskie*) is a different legal status from
any residence permit covered elsewhere in this category. A temporary or
permanent residence permit lets you live in Poland; citizenship is a
separate status decided under its own rules, by different institutions, and
it is what a Polish passport actually depends on.

Official sources describe **two main routes** for a foreigner who does not
already hold Polish citizenship:

**Recognition as a Polish citizen** (*uznanie za obywatela polskiego*) is
the more structured route: official sources list a set of specific
conditions, and meeting at least one of them lets you apply. Examples
described in official sources include continuous legal residence in Poland
for a set number of years with stable income and housing, being married to
a Polish citizen for several years combined with a period of residence,
having recognized refugee status for a set period, being a minor child with
one Polish parent, or having Polish origin combined with a Karta Polaka or
similar basis. Most adult applicants are also asked for a B1-level Polish
language certificate. The application goes to the **voivodeship governor's
office**, official sources describe a decision within about two months, and
an unfavourable decision can be appealed to the **Ministry of the Interior
and Administration (MSWiA)** within a set number of days.

**Grant of citizenship by the President** (*nadanie obywatelstwa polskiego*)
is a separate, discretionary route: official sources describe it as open to
people who do not hold Polish citizenship, without the fixed conditions
used for recognition. The application is submitted to a voivodeship office
if you live in Poland, or to a consulate if you live abroad, but the actual
decision is made by the **President of the Republic of Poland**, with no
statutory deadline — official sources note this route commonly takes over a
year.

A **Polish passport** (*paszport*) is a downstream document, not a residence
document: official sources are clear that it can only be issued to someone
who is already a Polish citizen. Once citizenship is confirmed, a passport
application is submitted in person — at a passport office (voivodeship
office) in Poland, or at a consulate abroad — along with an existing ID
document or passport if you have one, a recent photo, and proof of the fee
paid; official sources describe collecting biometric data (fingerprints) as
part of the in-person visit.

Because citizenship decisions are significant, individual, and in the
presidential-grant case not bound to a timeline at all, this page only maps
out the two official routes and where a Polish passport fits — it does not
suggest which route might apply to you. The official MSWiA and
obywatel.gov.pl pages below, or a qualified professional, are the reference
for your specific situation.
