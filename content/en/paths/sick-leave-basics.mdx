Two very different things happen to your income if you're sick, depending on
how you work.

**If you're employed under a contract of employment** (*umowa o pracę*),
sickness insurance is automatic and mandatory. Your doctor issues an
**e-ZLA** — the electronic sick note — which goes straight to ZUS and, once
processed, to your employer. Your **employer pays your sick pay directly**
for the first 33 days of incapacity in a calendar year (14 days if you're
over 50), generally at **80%** of your average pay from the past 12 months,
or 100% for specific situations such as a workplace accident, a
work-related illness, or pregnancy.

From day 34 (or day 15 if you're over 50), **ZUS takes over**, paying
**sickness benefit** (*zasiłek chorobowy*) at a similar rate, generally up
to a combined maximum of 182 days (270 days for tuberculosis or pregnancy).
Official sources describe a **30-day waiting period** of continuous
insurance before an employee is generally entitled to sick pay at all,
though exceptions exist — for example a workplace accident, or continuity
from previous insurance.

**If you work B2B or run a JDG**, none of the above happens automatically.
Sickness insurance for the self-employed is **voluntary**, not mandatory —
pension, disability, and accident insurance are compulsory, but sickness
insurance is a separate, opt-in choice made using the **ZUS ZUA** form.
There is no employer to pay you for the first weeks of illness; a B2B
contract is a civil-law arrangement between two businesses, not an
employment relationship, so it does not include sick pay by default. If
you haven't opted into voluntary sickness insurance, being sick generally
means no ZUS payment at all for that period — the only protection is
whatever your contract with the client happens to say.

If you have opted into voluntary sickness insurance, official ZUS sources
describe a longer **90-day waiting period** of continuous coverage before
sickness benefit becomes payable — compared with 30 days for employees —
reflecting that the insurance is optional rather than automatic.

Because contribution rules, rates, and time limits change periodically and
depend on individual circumstances, the official ZUS pages below are the
reference for your specific situation, including whether you are currently
covered at all.
